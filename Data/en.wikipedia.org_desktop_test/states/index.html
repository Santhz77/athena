<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Ukiyo-e" title="Ukiyo-e">ukiyo-e</a></b> genre of art flourished in Japan from the 17th to the 19th century. Its artists produced <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing" title="Woodblock printing">woodblock prints</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nikuhitsu-ga" title="Nikuhitsu-ga">paintings</a> of such subjects as female beauties, <a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">kabuki</a> actors and <a href="/wiki/Sumo" title="Sumo">sumo</a> wrestlers, scenes from history and folk tales, travel scenes and landscapes, flora and fauna, and erotica. The term <i>ukiyo-e</i> refers to pictures of the <i><a href="/wiki/Ukiyo" class="mw-redirect" title="Ukiyo">ukiyo</a></i> or "floating world" of kabuki theatre, <a href="/wiki/Oiran" title="Oiran">courtesans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geisha" title="Geisha">geisha</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Y%C5%ABkaku" title="Ykaku">pleasure districts</a>. Images of this environment became successful in the 1670s with <a href="/wiki/Hishikawa_Moronobu" title="Hishikawa Moronobu">Moronobu</a>'s paintings and monochromatic prints of beautiful women. By the 1740s, artists such as <a href="/wiki/Okumura_Masanobu" title="Okumura Masanobu">Masanobu</a> were using multiple woodblocks to print areas of colour. In the 1760s, with the success of <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_Harunobu" title="Suzuki Harunobu">Harunobu</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Nishiki-e" title="Nishiki-e">"brocade prints"</a>, full-colour production of prints made with numerous blocks became standard. Portraits of beauties and actors by masters such as <a href="/wiki/Torii_Kiyonaga" title="Torii Kiyonaga">Kiyonaga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utamaro" title="Utamaro">Utamaro</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sharaku" title="Sharaku">Sharaku</a> were prominent in the late 18th century. Masters from the 19th century include the bold formalist <a href="/wiki/Hokusai" title="Hokusai">Hokusai</a>, whose <i><a href="/wiki/The_Great_Wave_off_Kanagawa" title="The Great Wave off Kanagawa">Great Wave off Kanagawa</a></i> is one of the best-known works of Japanese art, and the serene, atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Hiroshige" title="Hiroshige">Hiroshige</a>, most noted for his series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fifty-three_Stations_of_the_T%C5%8Dkaid%C5%8D" title="The Fifty-three Stations of the Tkaid">The Fifty-three Stations of the Tkaid</a></i>. (<a href="/wiki/Ukiyo-e" title="Ukiyo-e"><b>Full article...</b></a>)</p>

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Dome_of_the_Rock" title="Dome of the Rock">Dome of the Rock</a></b> is an Islamic shrine located on the <a href="/wiki/Temple_Mount" title="Temple Mount">Temple Mount</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Old_City_(Jerusalem)" title="Old City (Jerusalem)">Old City of Jerusalem</a>. One of the oldest extant works of <a href="/wiki/Islamic_architecture" title="Islamic architecture">Islamic architecture</a>, the Dome of the Rock was initially completed in 691 CE at the order of <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">Umayyad Caliph</a> <a href="/wiki/Abd_al-Malik_ibn_Marwan" title="Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan">Abd al-Malik</a>. After the original dome collapsed in 1015, it was rebuilt in 1022–23, patterned after nearby Byzantine churches and palaces. The site has great significance for Muslims owing to traditions connecting it to the creation of the world and to the belief that the Prophet <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Isra_and_Mi%27raj" title="Isra and Mi'raj">Night Journey</a> to heaven started from the <a href="/wiki/Foundation_Stone" title="Foundation Stone">rock</a> at the center of the structure.</p>
