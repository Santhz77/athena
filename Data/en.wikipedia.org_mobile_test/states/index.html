<p>A <b><a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a></b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortified</a> structure, generally built in Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. Usually the private residences of lords and nobles, castles ranged from <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill forts</a> and country houses to expansive <a href="/wiki/Keep" title="Keep">keeps</a> surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Curtain_wall_(fortification)" title="Curtain wall (fortification)">curtain walls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fortified_tower" title="Fortified tower">fortified towers</a>. After the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a> in the ninth century, castles were used for defence, as bases for raiders, as centres of administration, and for controlling trade routes. As symbols of power, some grand castles had long winding approaches that dominated their landscape. In the late 12th and early 13th centuries, a scientific approach to castle defence emerged, leading to the proliferation of towers, with an emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Enfilade_and_defilade" title="Enfilade and defilade">flanking fire</a>. Many new castles were <a href="/wiki/Polygonal" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygonal">polygonal</a> or relied on concentric stages of defence that could all function at the same time. Castle building began to decline in the 15th century, when artillery became powerful enough to break through stone walls. In the 18th and 19th centuries, mock castles with no military purpose epitomized the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> revival of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival_architecture" title="Gothic Revival architecture">Gothic architecture</a>.
